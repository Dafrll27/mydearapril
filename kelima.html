You Finally Understand Your Worth

Published on 28 October 2025

In this blog, I will discuss the importance of setting boundaries to build healthier relationships and how to create proper closure.

What Are Boundaries?

Have you ever felt exhausted maintaining a relationship, whether it’s with a friend, a partner, a parent, a colleague or even your boss? Recently, I came across a book that has deeply helped my recovery process: Set Boundaries, Find Peace by Nedra Glover Tawwab.

Nedra Glover Tawwab, in Set Boundaries, Find Peace, emphasizes that the root of self-care lies in boundaries. The signs that we’re losing this root can actually be recognized in our daily lives: fatigue, feeling stuck in routines and a growing sense of resentment toward the people around us.

“Boundaries are expectations and needs that help you feel safe and comfortable in your relationships. Expectations in relationships help you stay mentally and emotionally well. Learning when to say no and when to say yes is also an essential part of feeling comfortable when interacting with others.” — Nedra Glover Tawwab

The book explains that we need to establish boundaries to build healthy relationships. Boundaries also serve as a signal that shows others how they should treat us.

Three Levels of Boundaries

1. Porous Boundaries

Porous boundaries are like a house whose doors are always open. Anyone can walk in, take what they want and leave whenever they please. From the outside, it looks kind and generous, but on the inside, the owner is exhausted.

2. Rigid Boundaries

If porous boundaries are too loose, rigid boundaries are too tight. It’s like a house with a locked door. People with rigid boundaries often avoid vulnerability and cut off relationships out of fear of getting hurt again.

3. Healthy Boundaries

Healthy boundaries are when we can open the door but also know when to close it. Healthy boundaries grow from self-awareness. They allow us to say “no” without guilt and “yes” without fear.

Assertive Communication

“The healthiest way to communicate your boundaries is to be assertive.”

Assertiveness means expressing feelings clearly without attacking others and without suppressing ourselves.

Assertiveness sounds like this:

“I’m not comfortable talking about that.”
“I appreciate your opinion, but I have a different perspective.”
“Thank you for inviting me, but this time I want to rest at home.”

Communication is not about winning arguments, but about giving others a map to understand us correctly.

Guilt Is Normal

When first enforcing boundaries, guilt almost always appears. That guilt is not a sign we are wrong — it is a sign we are leaving an unhealthy pattern.

“I’m not doing this to disrespect you, I’m doing this to respect myself.”

We can love someone and still say “no.” We can care and still keep distance.

When It’s Time to Let Go

“When you’ve tried setting boundaries and your requests are continuously violated, it may be time to consider cutting people off.”

Letting go is not a sign that we have stopped loving. It is a sign that we have started loving ourselves.

The Balance That Has Long Been Taught

“And do not make your hand chained to your neck (by being stingy), nor stretch it out completely (by being wasteful), so that you become blamed and regretful.” (QS. Al-Isra: 29)

In the end, as you might have realized since the very first blog:
Hurt doesn’t justify hurt.

Now you know one more thing:
Healing means learning when to walk away, not because you hate, but because you finally understand your worth.

Book Reviewed:
Tawwab, Nedra Glover. Set Boundaries, Find Peace: A Guide to Reclaiming Yourself. Penguin Life, 2021.
